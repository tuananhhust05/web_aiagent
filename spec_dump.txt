Strategy PRD: Backend Logic + Labels
1. Core System Flow
The backend should follow this sequence for every deal:
Read current deal context
Map current interest % to cognitive stage
Load stage-specific rules
Load active psychological guardrails
Detect current friction/blockers
Rank blockers by severity
Surface only the highest-priority blocker in UI
Generate recommended action, timing, and reasoning
Generate use-case scenario content
Update banner, deal list, and detail panel dynamically
This is the core intelligence architecture of the system.

===== strategry_feature/Guardrail Logic.docx =====
Complete Guardrail Logic System for Every Interest Transition Use-Case
System Architecture Overview
The guardrail logic system operates through a three-layer decision framework that ensures psychologically appropriate actions at every stage of the sales journey. The Rule Engine maps interest percentages to cognitive states, the Guardrail Activation Matrix determines which behavioral psychology principles are valid, and the Task Generator creates specific actions within those constraints. This architecture prevents the LLM from generating inappropriate recommendations while ensuring every task is grounded in proven neuroscience principles.
Complete Use-Case Logic Framework
USE-CASE 1: Cold Contact → Attention (0% → 10-20%)
Cognitive State Analysis:
 The prospect exists in a state of complete unawareness or mild recognition without engagement. Their brain’s default mode is defensive filtering, automatically categorizing unknown sales outreach as threats to be ignored. The amygdala is primed to reject anything that feels like a sales pitch. The psychological goal is to bypass threat detection and establish legitimate presence.
Primary Psychological Friction: Stranger-danger response and attention scarcity
Active Guardrails:
Credentialize Early: The first touchpoint must immediately establish why you deserve attention. Reference a mutual connection, specific company research, or relevant credential within the first two sentences
Schmooze First, Bargain Later: No business requests in initial contact. Focus on human connection points like shared interests, mutual connections, or genuine compliments
Lead with Honesty regarding Drawbacks: Mention a small limitation or qualification to signal authenticity and reduce skepticism

USE-CASE 2: Attention → Curiosity (10-20% → 20-30%)
Cognitive State Analysis:
 The prospect has noticed you and possibly consumed one piece of your content. They are in a neutral-to-slightly-positive state, evaluating whether you are worth further attention. The brain is asking “why should I care?” and “what’s in it for me?” The goal is to create an emotional or intellectual hook that makes them want to learn more.
Primary Psychological Friction: Relevance doubt and priority competition
Active Guardrails:
Diagnose the Customer’s Top Pains: Begin identifying whether their pain is financial (revenue loss), strategic (business risk), or personal (job security/stress)
Social Proof: Reference similar companies or roles to establish legitimacy
Lead with Honesty regarding Drawbacks: A small admission of limitation makes subsequent claims more believable

USE-CASE 4: Engagement → Evaluation (30-50% → 50-70%)
Cognitive State Analysis:
 The prospect has acknowledged they have a problem and believes your category of solution could address it. They are now evaluating whether your specific solution is the right fit and whether the investment is justified. The brain is in analytical mode, comparing options and building internal business cases. The goal is to establish value anchoring before competitors do.
Primary Psychological Friction: Solution skepticism and budget concerns
Active Guardrails:

Use Precise Rather Than Round Numbers: All data points should be specific (€47,350 not €50,000) to signal research depth
Explain Premium Pricing: Begin laying groundwork for why your solution costs what it costs
Avoid “Sushi Chef” Pricing: Present bundled, all-inclusive packages rather than itemized breakdowns

Cognitive State Analysis:

Primary Psychological Friction: Risk aversion and competitive comparison pressure
Active Guardrails:
Rejection-Then-Retreat (prepare): Be ready to offer a pilot program if full commitment faces resistance
Ensure Initial Demands are Reasonable: Your proposal must be ambitious but not extreme to maintain credibility
Soft Seats Reduce Rigidity: Physical comfort during key meetings reduces negotiation rigidity
Negotiate on Home Territory: Host critical meetings at your office when possible
Mirror the Prospect Discreetly: Match stakeholder communication styles in group settings

USE-CASE 6: Validation → Hard Commitment (70-80% → 80-90%)
Cognitive State Analysis:
 This is the critical transition point where the prospect has validated the solution but specific stakeholders still hold approval power. The goal is to eliminate the final friction points that prevent organizational commitment.
Primary Psychological Friction: Internal consensus challenges and authority concerns

Rejection-Then-Retreat (active): If resistance appears, immediately offer a smaller commitment (pilot program) to trigger reciprocity
Don’t Be Afraid to Apologize: Address any previous friction points directly and sincerely

USE-CASE 7: Hard Commitment → Decision (80-90% → 90%+)
Cognitive State Analysis:
 The prospect has committed internally but has not yet signed the contract. They are in a state of “decision made but not executed.” The brain is vulnerable to choice overload and second-guessing. Any new information or complexity can restart the evaluation cycle. The goal is to make the path to signature as frictionless as possible while reinforcing the decision already made.
Primary Psychological Friction: Decision fatigue and choice overload
Active Guardrails:
Limit Your Claims: Maximum three final value points. More than three creates choice overload and introduces doubt
Trigger Consistency with Questions: “What do you think?” forces them to verbalize commitment, creating psychological lock-in
Negotiate on Home Territory: Contract review meetings should happen at your office for home-status confidence
Favor the Right Ear: In face-to-face meetings, position yourself to their right when making final statements

===== strategry_feature/Signal Generation Dynamic way.docx =====
The signal generation should come from a hybrid model:
deterministic rules
LLM extraction
historical pattern learning
deal-context weighting
Do not hardcode:
pricing question = +7 forever
Instead do:
pricing question in first discovery call = +3
pricing question after demo = +8
pricing question with procurement involved = +12
pricing question after 30 days of silence = +1
The signal value changes depending on context.
1. Create Raw Signals Instead of Fixed Scores
Your agents should first produce raw evidence objects.
Example:
{
  "signal_type": "pricing_interest",
  "source": "meeting_transcript",
  "confidence": 0.84,
  "context": {
    "meeting_number": 3,
    "crm_stage": "demo",
    "speaker": "buyer",
    "persona": "procurement",
    "urgency": "high",
    "mentioned_with_budget": true
  },
  "quote": "Can you share detailed pricing tiers and annual discount structure?"
}
The LLM only extracts the signal and context.
The scoring engine later decides how important that signal is.
2. Use Multi-Factor Weighting
Every signal score should depend on multiple factors:
signal_score =
base_signal_weight
× stage_multiplier
× persona_multiplier
× urgency_multiplier
× recency_multiplier
× confidence_multiplier
Example:
Base pricing_interest signal = +5
If stage = Discovery → ×0.8
If stage = Demo/Proposal → ×1.4
If persona = Procurement → ×1.3
If urgency high → ×1.2
If confidence = 0.9 → ×0.9
Final:
5 × 1.4 × 1.3 × 1.2 × 0.9 = +9.8
So the exact same signal becomes stronger or weaker depending on the situation.
3. Add Signal Windows and Decay
Signals should lose value over time.
Example:
Asked for pricing yesterday = +8
Asked for pricing 14 days ago = +3
Asked for pricing 45 days ago = +0
You can use decay:
signal_{effective}=signal_{base}\times e^{-\lambda t}
Where:
( t ) = days since signal
( \lambda ) = decay rate
This prevents old positive meetings from making dead deals still look healthy.
4. Detect Compound Signals
Single signals are weak.
The system should detect combinations.
Example:
pricing question + implementation question + stakeholder invite
This is stronger than any one individually.
You can create compound-signal rules:
If pricing_interest + security_review + procurement_present
→ create "commercial_readiness" signal
→ +15
Another:
If competitor_mentioned + budget_pushback + delayed_next_meeting
→ create "deal_risk" signal
→ -18
This makes the system feel much smarter than simple keyword scoring.
5. Use Meeting History Patterns
Signals should compare current meeting behavior to prior meetings.
Example:
Meeting 1: only one stakeholder
Meeting 2: pricing mentioned
Meeting 3: procurement joins
Meeting 4: legal joins
This pattern implies increasing deal maturity.
Whereas:
Meeting 1: pricing interest
Meeting 2: silence for 21 days
Meeting 3: no new stakeholder
Meeting 4: repeated objections
This implies stalled momentum.
So instead of just scoring single events, score trajectories:
stakeholder_growth_rate
objection_reduction_rate
engagement_velocity
response_time_trend
meeting_frequency_trend
6. Use Historical Won/Lost Deal Learning
Eventually you should let the system learn from past deals.
For example:
In closed-won deals:
- pricing question usually appears in meeting 2-3
- procurement appears before meeting 4
- security review usually happens within 10 days of proposal
Then if a current deal follows similar patterns, the system increases interest confidence.
You can store embeddings of previous successful deal journeys in Weaviate and compare the current deal trajectory against them.
Example:
{
  "similar_deal_match": 0.81,
  "matched_pattern": "enterprise_security_review_before_procurement",
  "historical_win_rate": 74
}
Then the scoring engine can boost confidence.
7. Use Dynamic Rule Tables
Instead of a hardcoded table in code, store signal rules in MongoDB or Weaviate.
Example:
{
  "signal_type": "pricing_interest",
  "base_weight": 5,
  "multipliers": {
    "stage": {
      "discovery": 0.8,
      "demo": 1.2,
      "proposal": 1.5
    },
    "persona": {
      "champion": 1.0,
      "economic_buyer": 1.4,
      "procurement": 1.6
    }
  },
  "decay_days": 21
}
This allows sales ops or RevOps teams to tune signal importance without engineering work.
8. Suggested Signal Categories
You should not only detect "questions".
You should detect broader behavior types:
curiosity signals
urgency signals
budget signals
authority signals
stakeholder expansion signals
objection signals
trust signals
procurement signals
legal signals
risk signals
disengagement signals
champion signals
competitor signals

implementation readiness signals
Example:
"Can you send SOC2 docs?"
This is not just a security question.
It may create:
security_review_signal
procurement_readiness_signal
serious_buying_intent_signal
So one sentence can generate multiple signals.
9. Practical Pipeline
The actual runtime flow should be:
Transcript
→ LLM extracts evidence
→ Evidence converted into raw signals
→ Signals enriched with CRM context
→ Dynamic weighting engine computes score
→ Signal decay applied
→ Compound signals generated
→ Interest score updated
→ Next-best-actions generated
That way the system is dynamic, explainable, and tunable rather than just "LLM says deal is 72% interested".

Current interest % and cognitive state (e.g. "Trust, 58%")
